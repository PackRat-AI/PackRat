# Pacific Crest Trail Section Hiking: Best Segments for Every Skill Level

The Pacific Crest Trail spans 2,653 miles from Mexico to Canada through California, Oregon, and Washington. You do not need to thru-hike to experience its magic. Section hiking lets you taste the PCT's best offerings on your own schedule.

## Understanding the PCT Landscape

The PCT passes through dramatically different ecosystems:

- **Southern California (miles 0-700)**: Desert, chaparral, mountain passes. Hot and dry.
- **Sierra Nevada (miles 700-1,100)**: Alpine terrain, granite peaks, river crossings. The High Sierra is the crown jewel.
- **Northern California (miles 1,100-1,700)**: Volcanic landscape, forests, heat. Less dramatic but beautiful.
- **Oregon (miles 1,700-2,150)**: Gentle grades, lush forests, volcanic peaks. Easiest section of the trail.
- **Washington (miles 2,150-2,653)**: Rugged Cascades, rain, dramatic scenery. Most challenging section.

## Beginner Sections

### Crater Lake Rim (Oregon) — 33 miles
- Gentle terrain with stunning views of the deepest lake in the US
- Well-maintained trail with reliable water
- Best season: July-September
- Highlights: Crater Lake viewpoints, wildflower meadows, pumice desert
- Difficulty: Easy to moderate

### Cascade Locks to Timberline Lodge (Oregon) — 58 miles
- Follows the Columbia River Gorge up to Mount Hood
- Passes through old-growth forest and wildflower meadows
- Timberline Lodge offers a legendary all-you-can-eat buffet
- Best season: July-October
- Difficulty: Moderate (significant elevation gain to Mt. Hood)

### Big Bear to Wrightwood (Southern California) — 120 miles
- Desert and mountain scenery through the San Gabriel Mountains
- Well-spaced water caches (check PCT Water Report)
- Multiple bail-out points
- Best season: April-May or October
- Difficulty: Moderate (heat management required)

## Intermediate Sections

### Tuolumne Meadows to Reds Meadow (Sierra Nevada) — 60 miles
- Heart of the High Sierra
- Passes through the Ansel Adams Wilderness
- Dramatic views of the Minarets and Ritter Range
- Requires river crossing skills in early season
- Best season: July-September
- Difficulty: Moderate to strenuous (elevation 8,000-11,000 ft)

### Snoqualmie Pass to Stevens Pass (Washington) — 75 miles
- Classic Cascades scenery without extreme difficulty
- Alpine lakes, meadows, and mountain views
- Well-defined trail with moderate elevation change
- Best season: July-September
- Difficulty: Moderate to strenuous

### Mount Jefferson Wilderness (Oregon) — 45 miles
- Volcanic landscape with views of multiple Cascade peaks
- Jefferson Park is one of the most beautiful alpine meadows on the PCT
- Limited permits required for Jefferson Park
- Best season: July-September
- Difficulty: Moderate

## Advanced Sections

### Kennedy Meadows to Whitney Portal (Sierra Nevada) — 108 miles
- The crown jewel of the PCT
- Crosses Forester Pass (13,153 ft), the highest point on the PCT
- Includes optional Mount Whitney summit side trip (14,505 ft)
- Requires bear canister, ice axe skills in early season
- Best season: Mid-July to September
- Difficulty: Strenuous (sustained elevation above 10,000 ft)

### Glacier Peak Wilderness (Washington) — 80 miles
- Remote and rugged with limited bail-out options
- Multiple challenging river crossings
- Dramatic glacial scenery and alpine terrain
- Requires solid navigation skills
- Best season: August-September
- Difficulty: Very strenuous

### Goat Rocks Wilderness (Washington) — 32 miles
- The Knife's Edge traverse is one of the most exposed sections of the entire PCT
- Stunning views of Mount Rainier, Mount Adams, and Mount St. Helens
- Snow can linger into August on the high traverse
- Best season: August-September
- Difficulty: Strenuous (exposed terrain, potential snow)

## Planning Your Section Hike

### Permits
- A PCT long-distance permit covers any section over 500 miles
- For shorter sections, you need local permits (wilderness permits vary by area)
- Popular Sierra sections require quotas — book early
- Oregon and Washington generally have fewer permit restrictions

### Resupply
- Plan food carries of 3-7 days depending on the section
- Some sections have no resupply for 100+ miles (Sierra, parts of Washington)
- Kennedy Meadows General Store, Vermilion Valley Resort, and Muir Trail Ranch are famous PCT resupply points

### Transportation
- Most trailheads require shuttles or car placement
- PCT Trail Angels provide rides in some areas (do not rely on this)
- Public transit serves a few trailheads (Amtrak to Cascade Locks, buses to some CA trailheads)

### Water
- Southern California: water is scarce. Check the PCT Water Report (pctwater.com) daily.
- Sierra: abundant water from snowmelt (seasonal)
- Oregon: long dry stretches in some sections
- Washington: generally plentiful water

### Season Selection
- **Desert sections**: March-May or October-November (avoid summer heat)
- **Sierra**: July-September (snow dependent)
- **Oregon**: June-October
- **Washington**: July-September (weather window is short)

## Multi-Year Section Hiking

Many people complete the entire PCT over several years of section hikes.

### Strategy
- Tackle the Sierra and Washington first while physically ambitious
- Oregon makes excellent shoulder-season hiking
- Southern California sections work well for spring and fall trips
- Keep a trail journal and mileage log

### ATC/PCTA Recognition
- The PCTA recognizes section hikers who complete the entire trail
- Submit your completion application for a certificate and recognition
- No time limit on completion

## Conclusion

Section hiking the PCT lets you experience world-class trail without the 5-month commitment of a thru-hike. Start with an Oregon section to build confidence, tackle the Sierra for the experience of a lifetime, and save Washington for when you are ready for true wilderness. Every section of the PCT offers something worth the effort.
