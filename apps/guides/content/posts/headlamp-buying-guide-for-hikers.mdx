# Headlamp Buying Guide for Hikers

A headlamp is one of the ten essentials and the most practical lighting tool for the outdoors. Whether you are navigating pre-dawn trail starts, cooking dinner after dark, or reading in your tent, a reliable headlamp is indispensable.

## Understanding Lumens

Lumens measure total light output. More lumens means a brighter light, but brighter is not always better. High lumens drain batteries faster and can blind your companions.

**50-100 lumens:** Sufficient for camp tasks, tent reading, and well-marked trails at night. Excellent battery life.
**100-300 lumens:** The sweet spot for most hiking. Enough for trail navigation, cooking, and moderate-speed hiking after dark.
**300-700+ lumens:** Needed for fast trail running, technical terrain at night, or emergency signaling. Battery life drops significantly at these levels.

## Beam Patterns

**Flood beam** casts a wide, even light ideal for close-range tasks like cooking and map reading. Most headlamps default to a flood pattern.

**Spot beam** concentrates light into a narrow, far-reaching beam for trail navigation and identifying distant objects.

**Combination** headlamps offer both flood and spot, either as separate LEDs or an adjustable focus. This versatility is ideal for hikers who use their headlamp for varied tasks.

## Battery Types

**AAA batteries** are lightweight and universally available. A headlamp running on AAA batteries can be resupplied at any convenience store. Carry spare batteries on every trip.

**Rechargeable lithium-ion** batteries provide consistent brightness that does not dim as the battery drains, unlike alkaline batteries which gradually lose output. Most modern mid-range and premium headlamps use built-in rechargeable batteries with USB charging. The trade-off is dependence on charging access.

**Hybrid** headlamps accept both rechargeable and disposable batteries. This provides the best of both worlds: USB charging for convenience and disposable backup for long trips.

## Red Light Mode

Red light preserves your night vision and does not disturb sleeping companions. Most hiking headlamps include a red LED mode. This is genuinely useful for tent reading, midnight bathroom trips, and consulting maps without destroying your dark adaptation.

## Water Resistance

Look for headlamps rated IPX4 (splash-proof) at minimum. IPX7 (submersible to 1 meter) provides confidence in heavy rain. Most quality hiking headlamps meet at least IPX4.

## Weight

Ultralight headlamps weigh 1 to 2 ounces. Standard hiking headlamps weigh 2.5 to 4 ounces with batteries. Weight-conscious hikers can save an ounce or two by choosing smaller models with adequate brightness.
